# FlamingFoodies Editorial Style Guide

This document is the internal source of truth for how FlamingFoodies should sound.

Use it for:

- recipe generation
- blog generation
- manual editing
- editorial QA
- social caption review
- future prompt and automation work

## Brand promise

FlamingFoodies should feel like the warmest table in spicy food media.

We are not trying to sound like:

- a macho heat challenge brand
- a sterile SEO content farm
- a trendy restaurant newsletter
- a generic family lifestyle blog

We are trying to sound like:

- a trusted food-loving host
- a sharp home cook who has actually made dinner for other people
- a friend who can talk heat without making casual cooks feel stupid
- an editor who knows that food is about connection, not just ingredients

## Core voice

The best FlamingFoodies writing is:

- warm
- generous
- family-table oriented
- lightly opinionated
- practical
- specific
- welcoming to spice-curious readers
- confident without showing off

The best tone reference is:

- "I want to make this for people I care about."

## Audience posture

Write for readers who may love heat, but still cook for mixed tables:

- kids and adults
- spice lovers and cautious eaters
- weeknight cooks and weekend project cooks
- gift buyers and pantry builders

That means our writing should:

- respect different heat tolerance levels
- make recipes feel approachable, not exclusive
- offer off-ramps, swaps, and serving ideas
- help readers cook for a table, not just for themselves

## Tone rules

### 1. Warm over performative

Readers should feel invited in, not judged.

Good:

- "This is the kind of curry that makes a cold night feel sorted."
- "Keep the heat controlled at first, then let everyone add more at the table."

Bad:

- "Only real chili heads can handle this."
- "This will destroy your mouth in the best way."

### 2. Specific over generic

Use concrete textures, smells, moods, and use-cases.

Good:

- "The sauce should smell sweet and peppery before the chicken goes back in."
- "This bottle works best when rich food needs a sharp orange-citrus lift."

Bad:

- "Packed with flavor."
- "Perfect for any occasion."
- "Takes dinner to the next level."

### 3. Human over templated

Vary sentence rhythm. Let the writing breathe. Not every sentence needs to sound optimized.

Good:

- "You want the onions soft and a little sticky here, not deeply browned."
- "That last squeeze of lime matters more than another spoonful of heat."

Bad:

- "This flavorful dish comes together quickly and is perfect for busy weeknights."
- "The result is a delicious meal the whole family will love."

### 4. Family-table oriented over heat-theater

We can celebrate heat without making the entire brand about punishment.

Good:

- "Serve the hotter sauce on the side if you are feeding a mixed crowd."
- "The heat builds, but the dish still tastes like dinner, not a dare."

Bad:

- "This one separates the amateurs from the pros."
- "Use the hottest pepper possible unless you are scared."

### 5. Guidance over performance

The voice should help readers make decisions.

Good:

- "If you want this gentler, cut the chili paste in half and leave hot sauce at the table."
- "This is better for taco night than wings because the brightness keeps rich meat moving."

Bad:

- "Feel free to customize however you want."
- "There are endless possibilities."

## What we want readers to feel

After reading a recipe or blog post, the reader should feel:

- hungry
- capable
- included
- excited to share the page
- eager to save it for later
- confident enough to cook or buy something

## Recipe voice rules

Recipe copy should feel like calm coaching from a good host.

### Intros and hero summaries

They should:

- explain why the dish is worth making
- set the mood of the cook
- mention the real payoff
- sound like a person, not metadata

They should not:

- overstuff keywords
- overpromise
- repeat the title in dull paraphrase
- rely on empty praise

### Method writing

Method steps should:

- sound active and grounded
- include sensory cues
- reduce anxiety
- tell cooks what matters most

Best practice:

- tell the reader what to look for
- tell them what to avoid
- tell them why the step matters

### Tips and FAQs

These should:

- sound like hard-won kitchen guidance
- answer real family-table concerns
- anticipate leftovers, make-ahead, substitutions, and crowd heat tolerance

## Blog voice rules

Blog posts should feel editorial, not assembly-line.

They should:

- make a clear argument or recommendation
- help readers decide something
- connect spicy food to real life, meals, gifting, shopping, hosting, or culture
- include specific examples instead of abstractions

They should not:

- sound like encyclopedia entries
- bury the useful point
- restate the premise every few paragraphs
- use filler transitions to hit word count

## Social and referral voice

When writing socially shareable copy:

- make it feel useful enough to send to a friend
- favor "you should make/try/send this" over generic hype
- make family-table relevance explicit when true
- use warmth, curiosity, or practical surprise as the hook

Best hooks:

- solves a dinner problem
- makes a gift decision easier
- helps a mixed-heat household
- improves a familiar dish with one smarter move

Weak hooks:

- generic spiciness
- empty superlatives
- novelty heat for its own sake

## Words and phrases to avoid

Avoid or heavily limit:

- packed with flavor
- perfect for busy weeknights
- takes it to the next level
- bursting with flavor
- in all the right ways
- you'll love
- a flavor explosion
- game changer
- fiery feast
- flavor bomb
- mouthwatering
- whether you're
- the result is
- comes together quickly

## Things we should say more often

Use more language like:

- "good for a mixed table"
- "easy to finish at the table"
- "worth making ahead"
- "better with a little acid at the end"
- "the kind of bottle you actually use up"
- "heat you can still build dinner around"
- "sharpens rich food"
- "lets everyone season their own plate"

## Authenticity guardrails

Never invent:

- personal memories
- restaurant visits
- recipe testing stories
- interviews
- expert sourcing
- cultural authority we do not actually have

Instead:

- stay specific
- stay honest
- let the authority come from clarity and usefulness

## Editorial checklist

Before publishing, ask:

1. Does this sound like a real person who cooks for other people?
2. Would this help a reader feed a table, not just admire the idea?
3. Is there at least one sentence a reader would want to send to a friend?
4. Did we avoid generic food-writer filler?
5. Does the piece sound welcoming to both spice lovers and cautious eaters?

If the answer to any of those is "no", the draft needs another pass.
